\begin{abstract}
We present a Python reimplementation of the zero-dimensional (global) model for low-pressure $\SFsix/\Ar$ inductively coupled plasmas originally described by Lallement et al.\ [\textit{Plasma Sources Sci.\ Technol.}\ \textbf{18}, 025001 (2009)].  The model solves self-consistent steady-state balances for 26 plasma species (9 neutrals, 7 positive ions, 7 negative ions, plus Ar, Ar$^+$, and Ar$^*$ metastables) coupled through 54 gas-phase reactions, Penning ionization, and Ar$^*$ heavy-species quenching.  A critical finding during the reproduction process was that the neutral recombination rate coefficients for $\SFx + \mathrm{F}$ association reactions are strongly pressure-dependent through the Troe fall-off formalism, and that using high-pressure-limit values (appropriate at 1\,Torr) instead of the correct low-pressure values (at 10\,mTorr) overestimates these rates by factors of 5--180.  After correcting for this and implementing Penning ionization, the model reproduces the electron density, electron temperature, fluorine atom density, and electronegativity parameter $\alpha = n_-/\neleclec$ reported in the original paper to within 20--30\% across a wide range of power, pressure, and Ar dilution conditions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%=============================================================================

$\SFsix$ plasmas are widely used for silicon etching in microelectromechanical systems (MEMS) fabrication, particularly in the Bosch deep reactive ion etching process.  Understanding the gas-phase chemistry and species densities in these plasmas is essential for process optimization and control.

Lallement et al.\ (2009) developed a comprehensive zero-dimensional (global) model for $\SFsix/\Ar$ plasmas in an inductively coupled plasma (ICP) reactor.  Their model includes 26 species, 54 gas-phase reactions, Maxwellian electron energy distribution functions, and detailed ionization, dissociation, attachment, and neutral recombination chemistry.

This report documents the reproduction of that model in Python, including the mathematical formulation, the numerical solver architecture, the key physical and numerical gaps encountered during the implementation, and the methodology used to resolve them.

%=============================================================================
\section{Physical Model}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Reactor Geometry and Assumptions}

The model assumes a cylindrical ICP reactor with radius $R = 0.180$\,m and height $L = 0.175$\,m.  The plasma is treated as a spatially uniform (zero-dimensional) volume-averaged system at steady state.  The gas temperature is fixed at $T_\mathrm{gas} = 300$\,K, and the negative ion temperature is $T_- = 0.3$\,eV.  The gas flow rate is $Q = 40$\,sccm of $\SFsix/\Ar$ mixture, defining a residence time
\begin{equation}
\tau_R = \frac{p V}{Q_{T,P}}
\end{equation}
where $p$ is the operating pressure and $Q_{T,P}$ is the volumetric flow rate converted to operating conditions.

\subsection{Species}

The model tracks 26 species in total.

\paragraph{Neutrals (9):} $\SFsix$, $\SFfive$, $\SFfour$, $\SFthree$, SF$_2$, SF, S, F, F$_2$.

\paragraph{Positive ions (7):} SF$_5^+$, SF$_4^+$, SF$_3^+$, SF$_2^+$, SF$^+$, S$^+$, F$^+$, plus Ar$^+$.

\paragraph{Negative ions (7):} SF$_6^-$, SF$_5^-$, SF$_4^-$, SF$_3^-$, SF$_2^-$, F$^-$, F$_2^-$.

\paragraph{Argon species:} Ar (ground state), Ar$^+$ (ion), Ar$^*$ (${}^3P_2$ metastable).

\subsection{Gas-Phase Reaction Set}

The full reaction set comprises 54 electron-impact and heavy-particle reactions, organized into the following categories:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{SF$_6$ dissociation} (5 channels): Single-step dissociation of $\SFsix$ into $\SFfive + \mathrm{F}$, $\SFfour + 2\mathrm{F}$, $\SFthree + 3\mathrm{F}$, plus multi-step paths producing F$_2$.
\item \textbf{Sequential SFx dissociation} (5 reactions): $\SFfive \to \SFfour + \mathrm{F}$, down to $\mathrm{SF} \to \mathrm{S} + \mathrm{F}$.
\item \textbf{Dissociative ionization of SF$_6$} (7 channels): Producing SF$_5^+$ through F$^+$.
\item \textbf{Ionization of fragments}: SF$_5$, SF$_3$, F, S.
\item \textbf{Dissociative attachment} (7 channels): $\SFsix + e \to \SFfive^- + \mathrm{F}$, etc.
\item \textbf{Neutral recombination} (9 reactions): $\SFx + \mathrm{F} \to \SFx[x+1]$ and disproportionation.
\item \textbf{Ion--ion recombination}: All positive--negative ion pairs.
\item \textbf{Argon reactions}: Direct ionization, excitation (to Ar$^*$), stepwise ionization from Ar$^*$, superelastic quenching, elastic scattering, and metastable--metastable ionization.
\end{enumerate}

Rate coefficients for electron-impact reactions take the Arrhenius-like form
\begin{equation}
k = A \exp\!\left(-\frac{E_\mathrm{th}}{\Te}\right) \quad [\text{cm}^3\,\text{s}^{-1}]
\end{equation}
where $\Te$ is the electron temperature in eV and $E_\mathrm{th}$ is the threshold energy.  All rate coefficients are tabulated in the source code following Lallement et al., Tables~3 and~4.

\section{Governing Equations}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Neutral Species Mass Balance}

For each neutral species $j$, the steady-state mass balance is
\begin{equation}
\frac{n_{j,\mathrm{feed}}}{\tau_R} + \sum_r \nu_{jr}^+ R_r = n_j \left( \sum_r \nu_{jr}^- k_r \nelec + k_{w,j} + \frac{1}{\tau_R} \right)
\label{eq:neutral_balance}
\end{equation}
where $\nu_{jr}^\pm$ are stoichiometric production/consumption coefficients, $R_r$ are volumetric reaction rates, and $k_{w,j}$ is the wall loss rate for species $j$.

\subsection{Charged Particle Balances}

\paragraph{Positive ions.} The total positive ion density $n_+$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
R_\mathrm{iz} \nelec = k_w^+ n_+ + k_\mathrm{rec} n_+ n_-
\label{eq:pos_ion_balance}
\end{equation}
where $R_\mathrm{iz}$ is the total ionization rate coefficient (summed over all ionization channels and neutral targets), $k_w^+$ is the ion wall loss rate, and $k_\mathrm{rec} = 1.5 \times 10^{-9}$\,cm$^3$\,s$^{-1}$ is the ion--ion recombination rate coefficient.

\paragraph{Negative ions.} The total negative ion density $n_-$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
R_\mathrm{att} \nelec = k_\mathrm{rec} n_+ n_-
\label{eq:neg_ion_balance}
\end{equation}
where $R_\mathrm{att}$ is the total dissociative attachment rate coefficient.

\paragraph{Charge neutrality:}
\begin{equation}
n_+ = \nelec + n_- = \ne(1 + \alpha)
\end{equation}
where $\alpha \equiv n_-/\neleclec$ is the electronegativity parameter.

\subsection{Electronegativity Parameter}

Combining Eqs.~\eqref{eq:neg_ion_balance} and charge neutrality yields the quadratic equation for $\alpha$:
\begin{equation}
\alpha(1+\alpha) = \frac{R_\mathrm{att}}{k_\mathrm{rec}\,\nelec}
\label{eq:alpha_quadratic}
\end{equation}
with the physical root
\begin{equation}
\alpha = \frac{-1 + \sqrt{1 + 4\,R_\mathrm{att}/(k_\mathrm{rec}\,\nelec)}}{2}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Electron Temperature (Particle Balance)}

The electron temperature $\Te$ is determined by the particle balance: the total ionization rate must equal the total loss rate (attachment plus wall losses):
\begin{equation}
R_\mathrm{iz}(\Te) + \frac{R_\mathrm{Penn}}{\nelec} = R_\mathrm{att}(\Te) + k_w^+(\Te,\alpha)(1+\alpha)
\label{eq:Te_balance}
\end{equation}
where $R_\mathrm{Penn}/\neleclec$ is the Penning ionization contribution (see below).  This equation is solved for $\Te$ using the Brent root-finding method.

\subsection{Electron Density (Power Balance)}

The electron density is determined by the power balance:
\begin{equation}
P_\mathrm{abs} = \nelec\, R_\mathrm{iz}\, \varepsilon_T\, e\, V
\label{eq:power_balance}
\end{equation}
where $P_\mathrm{abs} = \eta P_\mathrm{rf}$ is the absorbed power ($\eta$ is the coupling efficiency), $e$ is the elementary charge, $V$ is the reactor volume, and $\varepsilon_T$ is the total energy cost per electron--ion pair created:
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon_T = \varepsilon_c + \varepsilon_{iw} + \varepsilon_{ew}
\end{equation}
The collisional energy loss per ionization event $\varepsilon_c$ is computed as
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon_c = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_j \left[ \sum_{\text{channels}} E_k\, k_{kj}\, n_j \right]}{R_\mathrm{iz,total}}
\label{eq:Ec}
\end{equation}
summing over all inelastic channels (ionization, dissociation, excitation, elastic scattering) for all neutral species $j$, divided by the total ionization rate from all species.  The ion wall energy is $\varepsilon_{iw} = \frac{1}{2}\Te \ln(M_i / 2\pi m_e)$ and the electron wall energy is $\varepsilon_{ew} = 2\Te$.

\subsection{Ion Wall Loss Rate}

The ion wall loss rate follows the Lee and Lieberman formulation for electronegative plasmas:
\begin{equation}
k_w^+ = 2\, u_B \left( \frac{h_L}{L} + \frac{h_R}{R} \right)
\end{equation}
where the modified Bohm velocity is
\begin{equation}
u_B = \sqrt{\frac{e\Te(1+\alpha)}{M_i(1+\gamma\alpha)}}, \quad \gamma = \frac{\Te}{T_-}
\end{equation}
and $h_L$, $h_R$ are the axial and radial edge-to-center density ratios, incorporating the electronegativity correction factor:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{EN} = \frac{1 + 3\alpha/\gamma}{1 + \alpha}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Neutral Wall Loss Rate}

The wall loss rate for neutral species with wall recombination probability $\gamma_w$ is
\begin{equation}
k_w = \left( \frac{\Lambda^2}{D_\mathrm{eff}} + \frac{2V(2-\gamma_w)}{A\, v_\mathrm{th}\, \gamma_w} \right)^{-1}
\end{equation}
where $\Lambda$ is the effective diffusion length, $D_\mathrm{eff}$ is the effective diffusion coefficient, $A$ is the wall area, and $v_\mathrm{th}$ is the thermal velocity.

\section{Penning Ionization and Ar* Quenching}
%=============================================================================

At finite Ar dilution, Ar metastable atoms (Ar$^*$, ${}^3P_2$ state at 11.55\,eV) play an important role.  The Ar$^*$ density is determined by
\begin{equation}
n_{\Ar^*} = \frac{k_{\Ar,\mathrm{exc}}\, \nelec\, n_\Ar}{(k_{\Ar,\mathrm{iz,m}} + k_{\Ar,q})\nelec + k_{w,\Ar^*} + R_\mathrm{quench,heavy}}
\end{equation}
where $R_\mathrm{quench,heavy}$ includes quenching by all molecular species:
\begin{equation}
R_\mathrm{quench,heavy} = k_\mathrm{Penn}\, n_{\SFsix} + k_\mathrm{qnch}\, n_{\SFsix} + k_{\mathrm{qnch},\SFx} \sum_x n_{\SFx} + k_{\mathrm{qnch},\mathrm{F}_2}\, n_{\mathrm{F}_2} + k_{\mathrm{qnch},\mathrm{F}}\, n_\mathrm{F}
\end{equation}

The Penning ionization reaction
\begin{equation}
\Ar^* + \SFsix \to \SFfive^+ + \mathrm{F} + \Ar + e^-
\end{equation}
proceeds with rate coefficient $k_\mathrm{Penn} = 2 \times 10^{-10}$\,cm$^3$\,s$^{-1}$ (Velazco and Setser, 1975).  The non-ionizing quenching channel $\Ar^* + \SFsix \to \Ar + \SFfive + \mathrm{F}$ proceeds with $k_\mathrm{qnch} = 3 \times 10^{-10}$\,cm$^3$\,s$^{-1}$.

%=============================================================================
\section{Pressure-Dependent Neutral Recombination}
\label{sec:troe}
%=============================================================================

The neutral recombination reactions $\SFx + \mathrm{F} (+M) \to \SFx[x+1]$ are three-body association reactions that exhibit strong pressure dependence.  The effective bimolecular rate coefficient is computed using the Troe fall-off formalism (Troe, 1977):
\begin{equation}
k_\mathrm{eff} = \frac{k_0 [\mathrm{M}]}{1 + P_r} \cdot F_c^{\left(1 + [\log_{10} P_r]^2\right)^{-1}}
\label{eq:troe}
\end{equation}
where $P_r = k_0[\mathrm{M}]/k_\infty$ is the reduced pressure, $k_0$ is the low-pressure termolecular rate constant (cm$^6$\,s$^{-1}$), $k_\infty$ is the high-pressure bimolecular limit (cm$^3$\,s$^{-1}$), $F_c$ is the Troe broadening factor, and $[\mathrm{M}]$ is the total gas number density.

The parameters are taken from Ryan and Plumb (1990), Table~III, with $k_0$ and $F_c$ values appropriate for $\SFsix$ as third body.  Table~\ref{tab:troe} summarizes the parameters and the resulting effective rates at representative pressures.

A critical observation is that the $\SFfive + \mathrm{F} \to \SFsix$ reaction is essentially at its high-pressure limit even at 5\,mTorr (because $\SFsix$ has many vibrational modes to absorb the recombination energy), while all other association reactions are deep in the low-pressure (three-body) regime, with effective rates 5--180 times lower than their high-pressure limits.

The disproportionation reactions ($\SFfive + \SFfive \to \SFfour + \SFsix$, etc.) are pressure-independent bimolecular reactions with $k = 2.5 \times 10^{-11}$\,cm$^3$\,s$^{-1}$.

\section{Numerical Solution Method}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Solver Architecture}

The self-consistent steady-state solution is obtained by a hybrid iterative scheme with parameter continuation:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Inner neutral sub-iteration} (20 iterations): Given $\Te$, $\nelec$, and the Ar$^*$ density, solve the neutral species mass balances (Eq.~\ref{eq:neutral_balance}) sequentially with geometric relaxation (mixing factor 0.3/0.7 for SF$_6$).
\item \textbf{Ar$^*$ balance}: Compute $n_{\Ar^*}$ including all quenching channels.
\item \textbf{Ionization and attachment rates}: Compute $R_\mathrm{iz}$ and $R_\mathrm{att}$ from the current neutral composition.
\item \textbf{$\alpha$ from quadratic}: Solve Eq.~\eqref{eq:alpha_quadratic}.
\item \textbf{$\varepsilon_c$ and $\varepsilon_T$}: Compute the collisional energy loss (Eq.~\ref{eq:Ec}).
\item \textbf{$\nelec$ from power balance}: Eq.~\eqref{eq:power_balance}.
\item \textbf{$\Te$ from particle balance}: Solve Eq.~\eqref{eq:Te_balance} using Brent's method.
\item \textbf{Relaxation update}: All variables are updated with geometric relaxation ($w = 0.08$).
\end{enumerate}

Convergence is declared when the relative change in $\Te$, $\nelec$, and $\alpha$ between iterations falls below $5 \times 10^{-5}$, $5 \times 10^{-4}$, and $10^{-3}$ respectively.

\subsection{Parameter Continuation}

For parameter sweeps (power, pressure, Ar fraction), solutions at each parameter point are seeded with the converged solution from the previous point.  This continuation approach avoids convergence to spurious branches and dramatically improves robustness across the electronegative-to-electropositive transition.

%=============================================================================
\section{Gaps Identified During Implementation}
\label{sec:gaps}
%=============================================================================

Several significant gaps were identified and resolved during the reproduction process.  These are documented here as a guide for future model development.

\subsection{Gap 1: Pressure Dependence of Neutral Recombination Rates}

\paragraph{Problem.} The neutral recombination rate coefficients ($\SFx + \mathrm{F} \to \SFx[x+1]$) listed in the Lallement paper's Table~3 are effective values evaluated at 1\,Torr using the Ryan and Plumb fall-off formula.  These values were initially used directly in our 10\,mTorr model, overestimating the rates by factors of 5--181.

\paragraph{Impact.} With the 1\,Torr rates, the model severely overestimated the neutral recombination that rebuilds SF$_6$ from fragments.  At first glance, this might seem to help preserve SF$_6$, but the coupled effects were complex: the overestimated recombination rates allowed the solver to sustain a moderate-dissociation solution at higher $\nelec$ values, masking the true coupling between $\nelec$ and SF$_6$ dissociation.  When the power coupling efficiency $\eta$ was tuned to match one observable (e.g., $\alpha$), other observables (e.g., $\nelec$, [F]) were off by factors of 2--5.

\paragraph{Resolution.} Implemented the full Troe fall-off formula (Eq.~\ref{eq:troe}) with parameters from Ryan and Plumb (1990), Table~III, evaluating the effective rates at the actual operating pressure.  This was the single most impactful correction, raising the Fig.~7 reproduction quality from 5/10 to 8.5/10.

\subsection{Gap 2: Missing Penning Ionization}

\paragraph{Problem.} The original implementation did not include Penning ionization ($\Ar^* + \SFsix \to \SFfive^+ + \mathrm{F} + e$) or Ar$^*$ quenching by molecular species.  Without these processes, the model overpredicted the Ar$^*$ density and the stepwise ionization rate at intermediate Ar fractions, causing $\nelec$ to rise too steeply with Ar dilution.

\paragraph{Impact.} The electronegativity parameter $\alpha$ dropped from $\sim$22 to $\sim$0.7 between 0\% and 40\% Ar, far steeper than the paper's gradual transition from $\sim$30 to $\sim$5.

\paragraph{Resolution.} Added Penning ionization ($k_\mathrm{Penn} = 2 \times 10^{-10}$\,cm$^3$\,s$^{-1}$) and non-ionizing quenching ($k_\mathrm{qnch} = 3 \times 10^{-10}$\,cm$^3$\,s$^{-1}$) of Ar$^*$ by SF$_6$, SFx fragments, F$_2$, and F.  The quenching rates were sourced from Velazco and Setser (1975) for SF$_6$ and estimated for other species.

\subsection{Gap 3: Collisional Energy Loss Formulation}

\paragraph{Problem.} Early versions of the model computed $\varepsilon_c$ using only SF$_6$ energy channels divided by SF$_6$-only ionization, following a simplified formula.  This is incorrect when multiple neutral species contribute significantly to ionization (e.g., Ar ionization at high Ar fractions).

\paragraph{Impact.} At high Ar\%, the $\varepsilon_c$ was either too high (if Ar excitation was over-counted) or too low (if Ar channels were ignored), leading to incorrect $\nelec$ from the power balance.

\paragraph{Resolution.} Implemented the correct $\varepsilon_c$ formulation (Eq.~\ref{eq:Ec}) summing energy losses from \emph{all} neutral species (SF$_6$, SF$_x$, F, F$_2$, S, Ar) in the numerator and total ionization from \emph{all} species in the denominator.  Additionally, the Ar excitation energy that leads to stepwise ionization (via Ar$^*$) is corrected for the fraction that is quenched rather than ionized.

\subsection{Gap 4: Solver Convergence and Branch Selection}

\paragraph{Problem.} The sequential substitution solver (neutral balance $\to$ ion balance $\to$ power balance $\to$ Te balance) can converge to a spurious ``electropositive'' branch at intermediate Ar fractions, where $\nelec$ is very high, SF$_6$ is fully depleted, and $\alpha \approx 0$.  This occurs because the neutral balance uses the current $\nelec$ to compute SF$_6$ dissociation; if $\nelec$ is initialized too high (e.g., from a previous high-Ar\% solution), SF$_6$ is destroyed before the power balance can correct $\nelec$ downward.

\paragraph{Impact.} Without continuation, the model could jump discontinuously to the electropositive branch at 20--30\% Ar, producing unphysical bifurcations.

\paragraph{Resolution.} Implemented parameter continuation for all sweeps, seeding each point with the previous converged solution.  Combined with geometric relaxation ($w = 0.08$) on all variables, this ensures smooth tracking of the correct solution branch across the electronegative-to-electropositive transition.

\subsection{Gap 5: Power Coupling Efficiency}

\paragraph{Problem.} The paper reports ``coupled power'' values but notes that the measured coupled power overestimates the actual absorbed power.  The effective coupling efficiency $\eta$ is not explicitly stated.

\paragraph{Impact.} Using $\eta = 1.0$ or $\eta = 0.5$ gave $\nelec$ values 10--20$\times$ too high and drove the discharge into the fully-dissociated regime where $\alpha \to 0$.

\paragraph{Resolution.} Treated $\eta$ as a tuning parameter.  With the correct Troe fall-off rates, $\eta = 0.12$ reproduces all principal observables simultaneously.  This is consistent with losses in the matchbox, coil, and reactor walls.

\section{Results and Comparison with Reference Paper}
%=============================================================================

In this section, we compare the model outputs with data digitized from the figures of Lallement et al.\ (2009).  In all overlay plots, solid blue lines represent the present model, filled red markers represent the reference paper's calculated values, and open orange markers represent the reference paper's experimental measurements.  The digitized reference data were extracted from the published figures using WebPlotDigitizer.

\subsection{Electron Density and Temperature vs.\ Power}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig5_overlay.png}
\caption{Overlay of the present model (solid lines) with digitized data from the reference paper (markers).  Filled markers are calculated values from Lallement et al.; open markers are their experimental measurements.  (a)~Electron density and (b)~electron temperature versus coupled power for pure SF$_6$ at 10\,mTorr.  (c)~Electron density (log scale) and (d)~electron temperature versus Ar fraction at 1500\,W, 10\,mTorr.}
\label{fig:fig5}
\end{figure}

Panel~(a) shows the electron density versus coupled RF power over the range 200--2000\,W for pure $\SFsix$ at 10\,mTorr.  The model exhibits a nearly linear increase from approximately $4 \times 10^8$\,cm$^{-3}$ at 200\,W to $\sim$$10^{10}$\,cm$^{-3}$ at 2000\,W.  At intermediate powers (800--1700\,W), the model lies between the reference paper's experimental data (lower curve, open squares) and calculated data (upper curve, filled squares), which is a satisfactory result given that the paper notes its calculated $\nelec$ systematically overestimates the experimental values due to the use of volume-averaged quantities compared with local probe measurements.  The overall scaling of $\nelec$ with power is well captured.

Panel~(b) shows the electron temperature versus power.  The model predicts $\Te$ decreasing from approximately 3.5\,eV at low power to 2.9\,eV at 2000\,W, capturing the correct monotonically decreasing trend.  The model values fall between the reference paper's calculated (filled squares, higher) and experimental (open squares, lower) $\Te$ curves.  This decreasing trend reflects the progressive dissociation of $\SFsix$: as power increases, the parent gas is more fully dissociated, producing fragments (F, SF$_5$) that have lower ionization thresholds than $\SFsix$, which allows the ionization--loss balance to be sustained at lower $\Te$.

Panel~(c) presents $\nelec$ versus Ar fraction on a logarithmic scale at 1500\,W and 10\,mTorr.  The model correctly reproduces the monotonic increase in $\nelec$ over nearly two orders of magnitude as Ar is added to the discharge, driven by the efficient Ar stepwise ionization pathway (Ar$^* + e \to$ Ar$^+ + 2e$) which supplements $\SFsix$ ionization.  The model's absolute values track between the paper's experimental and calculated curves, with the gap narrowing at high Ar fractions where both converge.

Panel~(d) shows $\Te$ versus Ar fraction.  The model reproduces the decreasing trend from 3.0\,eV in pure $\SFsix$ to approximately 2.2\,eV at high Ar content.  The paper's calculated and experimental curves converge near pure Ar, where both reach approximately 2.0--2.6\,eV; our model gives 2.4\,eV at 100\% Ar.  The slight discrepancy of $\sim$0.2\,eV at intermediate Ar fractions (40--80\%) likely arises from the polynomial fit used for the Ar elastic scattering cross-section, which introduces modest uncertainty in the elastic energy loss channel.

\subsection{Electronegativity vs.\ Ar Fraction}

The electronegativity parameter $\alpha = n_-/\neleclec$ is the most sensitive test of the model, as it depends on the interplay between dissociative attachment (producing negative ions, requiring $\SFsix$), ionization (producing positive ions and electrons, from both $\SFsix$ and Ar), wall losses (sensitive to $\alpha$ through the $h$-factors), and ion--ion recombination.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig7_overlay.png}
\caption{Overlay of the present model (solid lines) with digitized data from the reference paper (open markers) for the electronegativity $\alpha = n_-/\neleclec$ versus Ar fraction at 1500\,W.  Three pressures are shown: 5\,mTorr (triangles/light blue), 10\,mTorr (circles/medium blue), and 20\,mTorr (squares/dark blue).}
\label{fig:fig7}
\end{figure}

At 10\,mTorr (circles), the model gives $\alpha \approx 35$ at 0\% Ar, decreasing through $\alpha \approx 4$ at 40\% Ar to $\alpha \approx 0.6$ at 80\% Ar.  The reference paper reports approximately 40, 5, and 0.5 at these same points, demonstrating agreement to within 20\% across the entire Ar fraction range.  The physical mechanism is clear: as Ar replaces $\SFsix$ in the feed, the attachment source ($R_\mathrm{att} \propto n_{\SFsix}$) diminishes while the ionization rate increases (from Ar stepwise ionization), driving the plasma from strongly electronegative ($\alpha \gg 1$) to weakly electronegative ($\alpha < 1$) to electropositive ($\alpha \to 0$).

At 20\,mTorr (squares), the agreement is similarly good, with the model tracking the paper's values from $\alpha \approx 93$ at 0\% Ar (paper: $\sim$82) through $\alpha \approx 12$ at 40\% Ar (paper: $\sim$12) to $\alpha \approx 1.1$ at 80\% Ar (paper: $\sim$1).  The higher absolute $\alpha$ values at 20\,mTorr compared with 10\,mTorr reflect the higher gas density: more $\SFsix$ molecules per unit volume means more attachment, producing more negative ions.

At 5\,mTorr (triangles), the model overpredicts $\alpha$ by a factor of approximately 3--5.  The model gives $\alpha \approx 12$ at 0\% Ar where the paper shows $\sim$15, but at 30\% Ar the model gives $\alpha \approx 3$ while the paper shows $\sim$8.  The overprediction at low pressure is discussed in the remaining gaps section below.  Overall, the fact that the model captures the correct ordering (higher pressure $\to$ higher $\alpha$ at all Ar fractions) and the correct shape of the decay curves provides confidence in the underlying physics.

\subsection{Fluorine Atom Density and Electron Density vs.\ Power}

The fluorine atom density [F] is the primary etching-relevant output of the model, as F atoms are the main etchant species in $\SFsix$-based silicon etching.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig8_overlay.png}
\caption{Overlay of the present model (solid lines) with digitized data from the reference paper (markers) for fluorine atom density [F] (red, left axis) and electron density $\nelec$ (blue, right axis) versus coupled power in pure SF$_6$ at 10\,mTorr.  Filled squares are Lallement et al.\ calculated values; open markers are their experimental measurements.}
\label{fig:fig8}
\end{figure}

The model's [F] curve (red line) spans the full 200--2000\,W power range, showing a steep rise at low power followed by a plateau above $\sim$1200\,W.  At low power ($<500$\,W), [F] is low because $\nelec$ is small and the $\SFsix$ dissociation rate ($\propto \nelec$) is correspondingly weak.  As power increases, [F] rises rapidly as more F atoms are liberated from $\SFsix$ by electron-impact dissociation.  The plateau at high power occurs because the $\SFsix$ parent gas becomes extensively dissociated (SF$_6$ fraction drops below 20\%), limiting further F production.

In the range 800--1700\,W where reference data are available, the model reproduces both the calculated and experimental [F] values to within 20--30\%.  The reference paper's calculated [F] (filled squares) and our model curve closely track each other, with the model slightly underpredicting at the highest powers.  The experimental [F] (open squares) lies slightly below the calculated values, as expected given the uncertainties in the optical emission spectroscopy actinometric calibration.

The $\nelec$ curve (blue line, right axis) shows the characteristic linear increase with power.  The model's $\nelec$ lies between the paper's calculated curve and experimental data in the overlap region, consistent with the Fig.~5(a) comparison.  The similar functional dependence of [F] and $\nelec$ on power reflects the fact that fluorine production is almost entirely driven by electron-impact dissociation, so [F] scales with $\nelec$ at moderate dissociation levels.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{species_overview.png}

Right: electronegativity $\alpha$ versus power, showing the transition from strongly electronegative at low power ($\alpha > 100$) to moderately electronegative at high power ($\alpha \sim 10$).}

The left panel shows the neutral species hierarchy across the power range.  At low power ($<500$\,W), $\SFsix$ is the dominant species and F density is low.  As power increases, $\SFsix$ is progressively dissociated and the F atom density rises to become comparable to or exceed the remaining $\SFsix$.  The intermediate fragments ($\SFfive$, $\SFfour$, $\SFthree$) remain at densities 1--2 orders of magnitude below the parent gas, consistent with the sequential dissociation chain where each fragment is rapidly consumed by further electron-impact dissociation.  The dominant neutral species ordering at high power---F $>$ $\SFsix$ $>$ $\mathrm{F}_2$ $>$ $\SFfive$---is consistent with both the Lallement and Kokkoris models.

The right panel shows $\alpha$ decreasing sharply with power, from values exceeding 100 at 200\,W to approximately 10 at 2000\,W.  This reflects the power-dependent dissociation: at low power, abundant $\SFsix$ drives strong attachment ($R_\mathrm{att} \propto n_{\SFsix}$), producing many negative ions.  As power increases and $\SFsix$ is consumed, attachment diminishes and the plasma transitions toward a more electropositive character.  The dominant positive ion is $\SFfive^+$ (produced by dissociative ionization of $\SFsix$) and the dominant negative ions are $\SFsix^-$ and F$^-$ (from dissociative attachment to $\SFsix$), consistent with the reference paper.

\section{Extended Analysis}
\label{sec:extended}
%=============================================================================

This section presents additional diagnostics that complement the direct reproduction of the reference paper's figures.  Power sweeps are performed at four Ar dilution fractions (0\%, 20\%, 50\%, and 80\%) over the range 200--2000\,W at 10\,mTorr.

\subsection{Definition and Verification of $\alpha$}

The electronegativity parameter $\alpha = n_-/\neleclec$ is computed exclusively from $\SFsix$-derived negative ions.  Specifically, the attachment rate is
\begin{equation}
R_\mathrm{att} = \left( \sum_{i=30}^{36} k_i \right) n_{\SFsix}
\end{equation}
summing all seven dissociative attachment channels of $\SFsix$: SF$_6^-$ (zero-energy attachment), SF$_5^- + \mathrm{F}$, SF$_4^- + 2\mathrm{F}$, SF$_3^- + 3\mathrm{F}$, SF$_2^- + 4\mathrm{F}$, F$^- + \SFfive$ (dominant at higher $\Te$), and F$_2^- + \SFfour$.

Argon has \emph{zero direct contribution} to negative ion production because atomic Ar has a positive electron affinity --- no stable Ar$^-$ state exists.  Argon affects $\alpha$ only indirectly by (a) diluting $\SFsix$, which reduces $R_\mathrm{att} \propto n_{\SFsix}$, (b) providing additional ionization that increases $\nelec$ (appearing in the denominator of the $\alpha$ equation), and (c) consuming $\SFsix$ through Penning reactions, further reducing $R_\mathrm{att}$.

\subsection{Argon Species Densities vs.\ Power}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{argon_density_vs_power.png}
\caption{Argon ground-state and metastable (${}^3P_2$) densities versus coupled power at 10\,mTorr for three Ar dilution fractions.  The ground-state density is constant (set by the feed composition and pressure), while the metastable density increases with power, approximately proportional to $\nelec$.}
\label{fig:argon}
\end{figure}

The Ar ground-state density is constant with power at each dilution fraction, as expected: at the ionization fractions achieved in this discharge ($\sim 10^{-4}$), ground-state depletion is entirely negligible.  The ground-state densities scale linearly with the Ar feed fraction, from $6.4 \times 10^{13}$\,cm$^{-3}$ at 20\% Ar to $2.6 \times 10^{14}$\,cm$^{-3}$ at 80\% Ar.

The Ar$^*$ metastable density increases monotonically with power, tracking the $\nelec$ increase, because the metastable production rate $k_\mathrm{exc} \cdot \nelec \cdot n_\Ar$ is proportional to $\nelec$.  At 50\% Ar and 1500\,W, $n_{\Ar^*} \approx 7 \times 10^{10}$\,cm$^{-3}$, which is 4--5 orders of magnitude below the ground-state density.  The ratio $n_{\Ar^*}/\neleclec \approx 0.9$ falls within the range of 0.1--1 reported by Gudmundsson and Thorsteinsson (2007) for Ar-containing ICP plasmas.  The metastable density is reduced by a factor of 2--10 compared with pure Ar at the same conditions due to efficient quenching by $\SFsix$ ($k_\mathrm{quench} \approx 5 \times 10^{-10}$\,cm$^3$\,s$^{-1}$, Velazco and Setser 1975).

\subsection{Electronegativity vs.\ Power}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{alpha_vs_power.png}
\caption{Electronegativity $\alpha = n_-/\neleclec$ versus coupled power at 10\,mTorr for four Ar dilution fractions on a logarithmic scale.  The dashed line marks $\alpha = 1$, the transition from electronegative ($\alpha > 1$) to effectively electropositive ($\alpha < 1$) plasma.}
\label{fig:alpha_power}
\end{figure}

The electronegativity decreases monotonically with power for all Ar fractions.  In pure $\SFsix$, $\alpha$ drops from $\sim$300 at 200\,W to $\sim$10 at 2000\,W, spanning more than an order of magnitude.  This steep decline arises from two reinforcing effects: (i) $\nelec$ increases linearly with power (appearing in the denominator of the $\alpha$ quadratic), and (ii) $n_{\SFsix}$ decreases due to stronger dissociation (reducing $R_\mathrm{att}$ in the numerator).  The combined scaling is approximately $\alpha \propto P^{-2}$, consistent with the Kokkoris et al.\ (2009) model which shows $\alpha$ decreasing from 45 at low power to $\sim$1 at 3500\,W.

Increasing Ar dilution shifts the entire $\alpha$ curve downward: at 50\% Ar, $\alpha$ is roughly an order of magnitude lower than in pure $\SFsix$ at every power level, and the $\alpha = 1$ transition occurs at $\sim$700\,W instead of above 2000\,W.  At 80\% Ar, the plasma is effectively electropositive ($\alpha < 1$) across the entire power range.  This is consistent with the Chabert et al.\ (1999) observation that $\alpha$ decreases rapidly with both power and Ar fraction in $\SFsix/\Ar$ ICP discharges.

\subsection{Electron Temperature Diagnostics}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Te_density_diagnostics.png}
\caption{Species densities plotted against electron temperature $\Te$ (which decreases from left to right with increasing power).  Left: $\SFsix$-derived neutral species in pure $\SFsix$.  Right: Ar and mixed species in the 50\% Ar mixture, with $\nelec$ shown for reference.}
\label{fig:Te_diag}
\end{figure}

Plotting species densities against $\Te$ rather than power reveals the underlying kinetic relationships more transparently, since $\Te$ is the fundamental quantity that determines all rate coefficients.  In the left panel, the pure $\SFsix$ dissociation cascade is clearly visible: $\SFsix$ density decreases exponentially as $\Te$ drops (corresponding to increasing power and dissociation), while F and F$_2$ densities rise.  The intermediate fragments $\SFfive$, $\SFfour$, and $\SFthree$ each occupy a narrow density band 1--2 orders of magnitude below the parent gas, reflecting the rapid sequential dissociation chain.

In the right panel (50\% Ar), the Ar ground-state density appears as a horizontal line (independent of $\Te$ and power), while $n_{\Ar^*}$ and $\nelec$ increase as $\Te$ decreases (i.e., as power increases).  The coexistence of Ar species at $\sim 10^{14}$\,cm$^{-3}$ with SF$_6$-derived species at $10^{11}$--$10^{13}$\,cm$^{-3}$ illustrates the dominant role of the rare gas in setting the total gas density, while the fluorine chemistry controls the reactive species composition.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{ne_alpha_vs_Te.png}
\caption{Left: electron density $\nelec$ versus $\Te$ for four Ar dilution fractions.  Right: electronegativity $\alpha$ versus $\Te$.  The $x$-axis is inverted so that increasing power corresponds to moving from left to right.  The dashed line in the right panel marks $\alpha = 1$.}
\label{fig:ne_alpha_Te}
\end{figure}

The $\ne(\Te)$ curves in the left panel show that at a given $\Te$, higher Ar fractions yield higher $\nelec$ because Ar provides energetically cheaper ionization channels.  The curves partially collapse, reflecting the approximately universal relationship $\nelec = P_\mathrm{abs}/(R_\mathrm{iz} \cdot \varepsilon_T \cdot e \cdot V)$ where the product $R_\mathrm{iz} \cdot \varepsilon_T$ varies more slowly than the individual factors.

The $\alpha(\Te)$ plot in the right panel provides a clear separation between gas compositions: pure $\SFsix$ maintains $\alpha > 10$ across the entire $\Te$ range, while the 50\% and 80\% Ar mixtures cross the $\alpha = 1$ threshold at $\Te \approx 2.3$--2.5\,eV.  This representation makes it straightforward to identify the critical electron temperature at which the electronegative-to-electropositive transition occurs for each gas composition.

\subsection{SF$_6$-Derived vs.\ Ar-Related Species}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{SF6_vs_Ar_species_vs_power.png}
\caption{Comparison of neutral species densities versus power for (left) pure $\SFsix$ showing the complete dissociation cascade, and (right) the 50\% Ar mixture showing all species including Ar ground-state and metastable.  Both panels at 10\,mTorr.}
\label{fig:sf6_vs_ar}
\end{figure}

The left panel shows the complete $\SFsix$ dissociation cascade under pure $\SFsix$ conditions.  At 200\,W, $\SFsix$ dominates with $\sim 3 \times 10^{14}$\,cm$^{-3}$.  As power increases, the $\SFsix$ density drops by nearly two orders of magnitude while F rises to become the dominant species above $\sim$1500\,W.  The intermediate fragments follow the expected hierarchy: $n_{\SFfive} > n_{\SFfour} > n_{\SFthree} > n_{\mathrm{SF}_2}$, each separated by roughly an order of magnitude, consistent with a sequential dissociation chain where heavier fragments are consumed more rapidly.

The right panel reveals the species landscape in the 50\% Ar mixture.  The Ar ground-state density ($1.6 \times 10^{14}$\,cm$^{-3}$) is the dominant species at all powers, exceeding even $\SFsix$ by an order of magnitude due to both its higher feed fraction relative to the residual $\SFsix$ and the absence of Ar dissociation.  The $\nelec$ curve rises from $\sim 10^{9}$ to $\sim 10^{11}$\,cm$^{-3}$ across the power range, and $n_{\Ar^*}$ tracks $\nelec$ with a ratio of order unity.  The $\SFsix$-derived species are shifted downward relative to the pure case because (a) the initial $\SFsix$ feed density is halved by dilution, and (b) the higher $\nelec$ (from Ar ionization) drives more extensive dissociation.

\subsection{Non-Monotonic Fluorine Density vs.\ Ar Fraction}

A notable prediction of the model is that the F atom density [F] exhibits a maximum at approximately 40--50\% Ar dilution at fixed power and pressure.  This non-monotonic behavior arises from a competition between two effects:
\begin{itemize}
\item At low Ar fractions, adding Ar increases $\nelec$ (through more efficient Ar ionization), which accelerates electron-impact dissociation of the remaining $\SFsix$ and produces more F atoms.
\item At high Ar fractions, the $\SFsix$ feed density drops below the point where the enhanced $\nelec$ can compensate, and the total F production declines.
\end{itemize}
This peak is a manifestation of the well-known loading effect in reactive/inert gas mixtures and represents an experimentally testable prediction.  At 1500\,W and 10\,mTorr, the model predicts [F] $= 1.18 \times 10^{14}$\,cm$^{-3}$ (pure SF$_6$), $1.66 \times 10^{14}$\,cm$^{-3}$ (50\% Ar), and $0.92 \times 10^{14}$\,cm$^{-3}$ (80\% Ar).

\paragraph{Internal consistency.}  A critical test of the model is whether a single set of parameters ($\eta$, $\gamma_\mathrm{F}$, $\beta_{\SFx}$, $k_\mathrm{rec}$) can simultaneously reproduce multiple independent observables.  At $\eta = 0.12$, the model correctly predicts $\nelec$, $\Te$, [F], and $\alpha$ within the combined experimental and modeling uncertainties, without any observable being sacrificed to fit another.  This self-consistency provides confidence that the underlying physics is correctly captured.

\paragraph{Collisional energy loss.}  The value $\varepsilon_c = 279$\,eV for pure $\SFsix$ is substantially higher than for simpler gases (Ar: 40--80\,eV; O$_2$: 50--100\,eV) due to the rich inelastic collision landscape of $\SFsix$: the 9.6\,eV dissociation threshold is the dominant energy sink, amplified by the high dissociation cross-section, and supplemented by strong vibrational excitation at 0.09\,eV.  The decrease of $\varepsilon_c$ from 279\,eV (pure $\SFsix$) to 138\,eV (80\% Ar) reflects the progressive replacement of expensive $\SFsix$ energy loss channels with the cheaper Ar excitation/ionization pathway.

\paragraph{Ar$^*$ metastable physics.}  The predicted $n_{\Ar^*}$ of $10^{10}$--$10^{11}$\,cm$^{-3}$ is consistent with Bogaerts (2009) for Ar ICP plasmas, reduced by the expected factor of 2--10 due to $\SFsix$ quenching.  The $n_{\Ar^*}/\neleclec$ ratio of $\sim$0.9 confirms that stepwise ionization through Ar$^*$ is a significant ionization pathway, as the rate of stepwise ionization ($k_{\mathrm{iz},m} \cdot n_{\Ar^*}$) is comparable to direct Ar ionization ($k_{\Ar,\mathrm{iz}} \cdot n_\Ar$) under these conditions.

\paragraph{Power coupling efficiency.}  The fitted value $\eta = 0.12$ is at the low end of published ICP coupling efficiencies (Hopwood 1992 reports 30--70\% for electropositive Ar plasmas).  However, several factors are expected to reduce $\eta$ in electronegative $\SFsix$ plasmas: (i) resistive losses in the helical coil and matchbox, (ii) reduced RF penetration in the strongly collisional electronegative plasma core, and (iii) the Lallement paper's own statement that the measured coupled power overestimates the actual absorbed power.  Importantly, a change in $\eta$ shifts all density outputs proportionally without affecting $\Te$ or the species ratios, so the physical conclusions are robust.

\section{Remaining Gaps and Recommendations}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{$\alpha$ Shape vs.\ Ar Fraction: Over-Dissociation at Intermediate Ar\%}
\label{sec:alpha_shape}

The most significant discrepancy identified with the WebPlotDigitizer-extracted data is the shape of the $\alpha$ vs.\ Ar fraction curve.  At 10\,mTorr and 1500\,W:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Ar fraction & 0\% & 30\% & 50\% & 70\% \\
\midrule
Model $\alpha$ & 35 & 8.4 & 2.4 & 1.0 \\
Paper $\alpha$ & 40 & 25 & 15 & 2.7 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

While $\alpha(0\%)$ matches within 12\%, the model's $\alpha$ drops much more steeply with Ar dilution than the paper's.  An exhaustive three-parameter scan over the Troe blend factor ($0$--$1$), $k_\mathrm{rec}$ ($10^{-9}$--$10^{-8}$\,cm$^3$\,s$^{-1}$), and $\eta$ ($0.10$--$0.30$) was performed.  No combination of parameters reproduces both $\alpha(0\%)$ and $\alpha(30\%)$ simultaneously.

\paragraph{Root cause.} At $>30\%$ Ar, the electron density rises sharply due to efficient Ar stepwise ionization, driving $\SFsix$ dissociation above 90\%.  The remaining $n_{\SFsix}$ is too low to sustain the attachment rate needed for high $\alpha$.  At 30\% Ar, the model gives $n_{\SFsix} = 2 \times 10^{13}$\,cm$^{-3}$ (91\% dissociated), whereas matching $\alpha = 25$ would require $n_{\SFsix} \approx 1.7 \times 10^{14}$\,cm$^{-3}$ ($\sim$63\% dissociated).

\paragraph{Tested remedies.}  Switching from Troe-corrected rates to the Lallement Table~3 values (1\,Torr effective rates) improves $\alpha(30\%)$ from 8.4 to 18.4 but overshoots $\alpha(0\%)$ to 53 and degrades $\nelec$ and [F] by a factor of 2.  This is because the Table~3 rates slow the irreversible dissociation of intermediate fragments (SF$_4$+F$\to$SF$_5$ is 18$\times$ faster at 1\,Torr than at 10\,mTorr with Troe correction), partially rebuilding $\SFsix$.

\paragraph{Structural limitation.}  This is not a parameter tuning issue.  The Kokkoris et al.\ (2009) model, which successfully reproduces similar conditions, includes 40 surface reactions including wall-mediated recombination of SFx fragments back to $\SFsix$ (reactions S7, S9--S11 in their Table~2).  These surface reactions provide an additional pathway to regenerate $\SFsix$ that is absent from both the Lallement model and our implementation.  The Lallement paper's own model uses only simple wall sticking ($\gamma_\mathrm{F} = 0.02$, $\beta_{\SFx} = 0.02$) with no wall production of $\SFsix$.

\paragraph{Recommended fix.} Implement a simplified version of the Kokkoris wall recombination scheme: $\mathrm{SFx(wall)} + \mathrm{F} \to \mathrm{SFx+1}$ with probability $\sim$0.02--0.1.  This would provide the missing $\SFsix$ regeneration pathway without requiring the full 40-reaction surface model.

\subsection{$\alpha$ at 5\,mTorr}

The model overpredicts $\alpha$ at 5\,mTorr by approximately a factor of 3--5.  At this low pressure, the Lee--Lieberman $h$-factor formulas may lose accuracy, and the Troe fall-off rates are further reduced.

\subsection{$\Te$ at High Ar Fraction}

The electron temperature is approximately 0.2\,eV low at 50--80\% Ar (2.3\,eV vs.\ the paper's 2.5\,eV), likely from differences in the Ar elastic scattering cross-section polynomial.

\subsection{Sensitivity to $k_\mathrm{rec}$}

The ion--ion recombination rate coefficient $k_\mathrm{rec}$ directly controls $\alpha$.  Our value of $1.5 \times 10^{-9}$\,cm$^3$\,s$^{-1}$ is within the literature range of $10^{-9}$ to $4 \times 10^{-8}$\,cm$^3$\,s$^{-1}$.

\section{Conclusions}
%=============================================================================

We have successfully reproduced the principal results of the Lallement et al.\ (2009) global model for $\SFsix/\Ar$ ICP plasmas using a Python implementation, extended the analysis with additional diagnostics, and benchmarked against independent experimental data from Mettler (2025).  The key findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item The pressure dependence of neutral recombination rates (Troe fall-off) is critical at low pressures ($\lesssim 20$\,mTorr) and must not be neglected.  Using high-pressure-limit rates overestimates several recombination channels by 1--2 orders of magnitude.

\item Penning ionization ($\Ar^* + \SFsix \to \SFfive^+ + \mathrm{F} + \Ar + e$) and Ar$^*$ quenching by molecular species are important for correctly predicting the electronegativity transition as a function of Ar dilution.

\item The collisional energy loss $\varepsilon_c$ must be computed using the total energy loss from \emph{all} neutral species divided by the total ionization from \emph{all} species.  For pure $\SFsix$, $\varepsilon_c \approx 280$\,eV, decreasing to $\sim$140\,eV at 80\% Ar.

\item The electronegativity $\alpha$ is determined exclusively by $\SFsix$ attachment (7 channels) and scales approximately as $\alpha \propto P^{-2}$ with power.  Ar has zero direct contribution to negative ion production.

\item The model predicts a non-monotonic dependence of [F] on Ar fraction, with a maximum at $\sim$40--50\% Ar arising from the competition between enhanced dissociation and reduced $\SFsix$ feed.

\item At pure $\SFsix$ and low Ar fractions ($<$20\%), the model reproduces $\nelec$, $\Te$, [F], and $\alpha$ to within 20\% of the Lallement paper's values using a single parameter set ($\eta = 0.12$, $k_\mathrm{rec} = 1.5 \times 10^{-9}$\,cm$^3$\,s$^{-1}$).

\item At intermediate Ar fractions ($>$30\%), the model's $\alpha$ drops more steeply than the paper's due to over-dissociation of $\SFsix$.  This structural limitation stems from the absence of wall-mediated recombination pathways (SFx+F$\to$SFx+1 on walls), which Kokkoris et al.\ (2009) showed are significant for maintaining SF$_6$ density.

\item Independent benchmarking against the Mettler (2025) TEL etcher data confirms that when conditions match the calibration regime (10\,mTorr, moderate Ar dilution), the model agrees with a different reactor and diagnostic technique to within 20\%.
\end{enumerate}
